Cultural Inclusion & Embedding Aboriginal Perspectives

A learning module for Georges River Libraries staff based on the key learnings from the 2025 Koori Curriculum event.

Core Principles for Authentic Cultural Inclusion

This module provides a foundation for enhancing your practice of cultural inclusion. It is guided by four core principles to ensure our approach is respectful, authentic and meaningful.

  • ✓

    Authentic, Embedded Practice

    Aboriginal culture should be woven into daily operations and programming, not treated as a separate "theme." It should be a holistic, everyday presence.

  • ✓

    Continuous Learning

    Cultural understanding is an ongoing journey, not a checklist. We are all continuously learning and should remain open to new insights.

  • ✓

    Community and Country-Centric

    Our practices should directly reflect the local Aboriginal community and the land (Country) on which we operate.

  • ✓

    Inclusivity of All Cultures

    While focusing on Aboriginal perspectives, our goal is to celebrate and embed all global cultures to ensure everyone feels a sense of belonging.

'Country' is a fundamental concept. It is not just land, but the waters, skies, animals, plants, and spiritual connections that have existed for millennia. Acknowledging Traditional Custodians is the first step in showing respect.

Practical Ways to Connect to Country:

  • Learn the Local Seasons: Research the local Aboriginal seasons (e.g., via the Bureau of Meteorology website) by observing changes in plants, animals, winds, and tides. This helps ground your programs in culture throughout the year.
  • Explore Local Ecosystems: Learn about the endemic plants and animals in your area. This enriches understanding of biodiversity and Indigenous cultures.
  • On-Country Learning: "Country" is everywhere, including urban areas. Simple "walk and talk" programs in a local park are a powerful way to connect with the history and traditional ownership of the land.

Welcome to Country can only be performed by a recognised Traditional Custodian of that specific Country. It is a cultural practice that has been performed for thousands of years to welcome visitors to their land.

An Acknowledgement of Country can be given by anyone, and it is a way of showing respect for the Traditional Custodians. It is common practice to give an Acknowledgement at the start of meetings, events, and significant gatherings.

Best Practice

Always research the name of the Traditional Custodians of your area. If you are unsure, contact your local Aboriginal Land Council or cultural centre for advice. It is vital to implement this culture correctly and respectfully.

A culturally safe collection actively promotes materials by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander creators and involves auditing existing collections for outdated or offensive content.

Key Strategies:

  • Storytelling as a Core Method: Use 'own voices' books to share Dreamtime stories and history, and to introduce complex topics like the Stolen Generations in a sensitive, age-appropriate way.
  • Ethical Sourcing: Ensure all materials accurately represent the culture. Prioritise 'own voices' authors and illustrators such as Shelley Ware, Corey Tutt, Gregg Dreise, and Aunty Joy Murphy.
  • Mindful Consumerism: Before purchasing new resources, consider if they are environmentally friendly, ethical, or can be borrowed. Aim for quality over quantity.

Meaningful engagement is built on genuine, lasting relationships, not transactional interactions. It's about listening and collaborating.

Ideas for Engagement:

  • Language Integration: Identify and prioritise the local Aboriginal language. Engage with language custodians to include words and greetings in storytime. Use QR codes in displays for audio pronunciations.
  • Environmental Advocacy: Empower children to be advocates. Create programs where they can identify local problems (e.g., litter) and propose solutions (e.g., making signs, "Take 3 for the Sea").
  • Connect with your feet: Visit local Aboriginal businesses and community members. Attend local Welcome to Country ceremonies and listen deeply to Elders.
  • Seek Permission: Always seek permission from custodians before embedding shared stories or cultural practices into your regular programming.

Every staff member has a role to play in fostering cultural safety and inclusion. Here are some ways you can contribute:

  • Be Open, Not Fearful: Don't be afraid to explore Aboriginal culture. Taking the first step leads to support and helps build future generations' connection to culture.
  • Facilitate, Don't Just Teach: Empower community members, especially children, to be advocates. Ask them how they want to solve problems and support their ideas.
  • Critically Reflect: Constantly evaluate activities to ensure they are genuinely fostering cultural understanding. Be open to changing practices based on new knowledge.
  • Build Knowledge to Build Respect: Invest time in learning with community members about local stories, flora, and fauna. Understanding fosters appreciation.

Reflection Question

Beyond a formal Acknowledgement of Country, what is one practical action your library branch could take this month to connect a program to 'Country' (e.g., learning about a local plant or season)?

Suggested Answer:

A library could theme a storytime around the current local Aboriginal season. This would involve researching the season, finding a related story by a First Nations author, and creating a simple craft activity using images of local flora or fauna that are prominent during this time.

Reflection Question

A colleague is hesitant to use the local Aboriginal language greeting in storytime for fear of "getting it wrong." How could you support them in a way that respects their concern but also encourages them to take a first step?

Suggested Answer:

First, validate their concern—it shows they care about being respectful. Suggest finding an online audio resource from a local language centre to hear the correct pronunciation. You could offer to practise with them and remind them that the community generally appreciates a genuine attempt. Starting with just one word, like "hello," is a great first step.

Reflection Question

Think about your library's junior fiction section. What steps could you take this month to begin a 'collection audit' to assess the quality and authenticity of its Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander representation?

Suggested Answer:

A first step could be to create a checklist based on recognised guidelines (like those from AIATSIS or the State Library). Then, I could spend a few hours spot-checking 10-15 popular or older titles against the checklist to identify common issues, such as outdated terminology or stereotypical portrayals. This initial sample would help build a case for a more thorough audit.

Reflection Question

A library wants to start a First Nations storytime. The manager suggests asking a well-known Aboriginal community member to volunteer. Why is it important to offer payment for this person's time, knowledge, and cultural labour? What does this demonstrate about moving from a transactional to a relational approach?

Suggested Answer:

Offering payment shows respect for their expertise, cultural knowledge, and time, acknowledging it as a professional service. It avoids placing the burden of "community service" on individuals who are already frequently asked to share their culture for free. This moves the dynamic from a one-sided transaction to a reciprocal, professional partnership, which is the foundation of a genuine and respectful relationship.
